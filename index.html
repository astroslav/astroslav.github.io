What I Can Do

Scientific Computing

During my master's, I used open-source software (MESA and GR1D) to create state-of-the-art simulations of black holes being born. Using Fortran, I wrote and edited scripts that were submitted to the high performance computing systems provided by Compute Canada to run large scale black hole simulations. I then visualized the results by analyzing the data using Python, FLASH, and VisIt.

Physics of Black Hole Formation in Failed Supernovae

My master's thesis explores failed supernovae and black hole formation. We don't have a good understanding of which massive stars die and which explode. This led me to program simulations that used general relativity, rather than the simpler Newtonian physics, to more accurately simulate this complex mechanism. The link to the paper is the (soon-to-be) published research article rather than my thesis.

X-ray Emissivity of Low-Density Stellar Populations

I am a main author of a published exploratory statistical study on the effects of binary fraction, metallicity, and density on the X-ray emissivity of various stellar environments. We sample our parameter space using Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods and create fits using bootstrap techniques.

"Where is everyone?" Under the Stars Keynote Presentation

I was the keynote speaker at the "Under the Stars" event in Bon Accord, Alberta. I gave a one-hour presentation on the millennium-old question of whether we are alone in the universe or not. The presentation covered topics ranging from the scale of the universe, to how likely it is that there is life elsewhere, to what some of the challenges are that humanity faces in space exploration, to what we are doing now to progress towards a spacefaring civilization.
