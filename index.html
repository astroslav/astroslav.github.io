About Me

I'm an astrophysicist by trade, but I'm highly interested in data science. I finished a M.Sc. in Physics with experience in both observational and theoretical astrophysics. I have experience with analyzing large data sets and applying modern statistical methods to find underlying patterns. I mainly work with Python, but I also used Fortran (for running big-data simulations), Bash (for general purpose programming and writing batch scripts for Compute Canada), and other open-source codes like GR1D and FLASH. Most of my time spent during my masters was spent using and making changes to scientific codes as well as diagnosing issues involved in this process.

My Skills

A list, in no particular order, of skills I possess.

Scientific Computing

In my current work, I use GR1D to run hydrodynamic supernova simulations, written in Fortran, that take into account neutrino heating/cooling and general relativity. I then visualize the results by either analyzing the data in Python or by using software such as FLASH or VisIt. I have also worked with MESA to generate the stellar progenitors required for my supernova simulations. In addition to familiarity with scientific programming languages I use the high performance computing systems provided by Compute Canada to do research.

Data Visualization

Using Python and its modules such as matplotlib, numpy and scipy I can create clear and useful figures from the huge quantity of data produced by simulations. I have experience in using and making changes to scientific codes as well as diagnosing issues involved in this process. I also have experience in creating scientific posters to present my research at conferences through PowerPoint and LaTeX. Examples of these posters and figures can be found in my research.

Teaching

I'm working as a teaching assistant during my master's degree at the University of Alberta. I currently mark assignments and answer questions related to 4th year undergraduate astronomy and physics courses. Previously, I was a teaching assistant for various years of physics and astronomy classes, including teaching 1st year astronomy students at the observatory about meteorites and objects they can see in the night sky. Beyond this, I have been a self-employed tutor, since 2015, teaching undergraduate physics, math, engineering, and all high-school natural sciences.

X-ray Emissivity of Low-Density Stellar Populations

An exploratory statistical study of the effects of binary fraction, metallicity, and density on the X-ray emissivity of various stellar environments. It also focuses on three literature claims of unusual X-ray sources. We sample our parameter space using Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods and create fits using bootstrap techniques.
